Watch Bands

February 7, 2020 2:00 PM

share

<[Insert Leather Strap Image](https://www.jomashop.com/hadley-roma-19aat10m.html)> **Leather Straps** The most traditional, ubiquitous, and popular choice of watch band is leather. There are many different types of animal hides used in the production of genuine leather straps with cowhide at the top of that list. Other popular animal leathers are crocodile, alligator, ostrich, lizard, and horse (used to make shell cordovan straps). Other less common (but kind of cool) types are giraffe, tree frog, and horn-back toad. But if you like the style of leather straps but object to the use of animal products, there is now a very serviceable alternative available. Some forward thinking manufacturers have started creating sustainable watch straps out of cork. The results are fantastic, and the future of these environmentally-conscious straps are bright. <[Insert Rubber Straps](https://www.jomashop.com/breitling-strap-bt142s.html)> **Rubber Straps** Certain activities lend themselves to rubber straps, but none more so than diving. Rubber straps are becoming increasingly common but do vary massively in quality. India rubber and FKM (fluoroelastomer category) rubbers are particularly nice on the wrist, but cheaper rubber straps can just cause you to sweat excessively. To combat this, many brands have started infusing their rubber straps with a strong scent of vanilla, which is delightful after a little time, but can be quite overpowering out of the box (especially if you’re unprepared for it). <[Insert Fabric Strap Image](https://www.jomashop.com/tudor-watch-79580-0005.html)> **Fabric straps** Fabric is a catchall term for any material that is not rubber, leather, or metal. There are many different types of fabric used in watchstraps these days, with canvas, kevlar, and nylon all making appearances. <[Insert NATO Image](https://www.jomashop.com/tissot-watch-t0954173711701.html)> **NATO and ZULU straps** NATO and ZULU straps are very nearly the same thing, differentiated by the shape of the metal hardware they use and the general thickness of the straps. ZULU straps (which come in three, four, or five ring varieties) tend to be thicker, are more often leather than NATOs are, and use rounded, usually brushed “loop” style hardware. NATO straps (which should really be called G10 straps because of their original inventory reference) were issued to military staff as part of their active duty kit. The way the G10 threads between the lugs and the case one both sides creates an unbroken loop and means that your watch will stay on your wrist even if one spring bar breaks. Handy for life or death situations for when you might need your watch to survive. G10 straps tend to made of nylon, are traditionally Admiral Gray in color (although are very popular in colorful stripes), and have low profile, angular hardware. <[Insert Bracelet Image](https://www.jomashop.com/breitling-bracelet-366a.html)> **Bracelets** Although the term bracelet may connote a piece of jewelry, it is in fact the catch all term for a metal “strap” when used on a watch. Bracelets come in several different styles. As with a strap, if you hear a bracelet described as “integrated” it means it fits exclusively to the watch it has been designed to fit to (check out the unusual end links of the Audemars Piguet Royal Oak for an example). Most bracelets are constructed with links, which a small, articulated pieces of metal that fit together like a jigsaw snake. It is possible to get expandable bracelets, which use spring-loaded links to ensure a perfectly snug fit on the wrist. These are notorious for ripping out arm hair, though, so if you are a particularly hirsute person it might be worth steering clear of those... <[Insert Ardillon Image](https://www.jomashop.com/breitling-229x-a20basa-1.html)> **Ardillon Buckle** The ardillon buckle may well have a fancy name, but it’s actually the most common type of buckle in the industry. It has almost certainly featured on a watch you’ve worn at some point in your life. The watch band used with an ardillon buckle is normally leather, rubber, or fabric. The strap fastening looks very much like a little belt. These types of buckles are also called pin buckles. <[Insert Deployant Image](https://www.jomashop.com/omega-watch-304-93-44-52-03-002.html)> **Deployant clasps** There are many different types of deployant clasps, all with slightly different names that are unlikely to be 100% agreed upon across the industry, but here are a few examples of the most common to start with. The fold-over deployant clasp is the friend of watchstraps the world over. The fold-over style means the strap is never under the same stress or strain as it is when using an ardillon buckle. It’s also a security mechanism, because once the clasp has been correctly fitted to the watch is forms an unbroken loop. Should you fumble your watch when attempting to put it on, it’s less likely to hit the deck. Another type of deployant is the butterfly clasp. This clasp folds out in _two_ directions instead of one. This is particularly good for skinny wristed people with big hands, because you’re able to massively increase the size of the bracelet when you’re putting it on. Most people would likely be able to don and doff a butterfly clasp by only undoing one half of it. There are many more types of buckles, and more and more straps varieties emerging every day. These elements of watchmaking are fun to dig into because they do increase you wearing comfort and thus your relationship with and the enjoyment taken from your timepiece. It’s worth experimenting and finding a combination that works for you.

Watch Materials: Steel And Gold

February 21, 2020 11:02 AM

share

**Stainless Steel** By far and a way the most common (and easily the most popular) material in watchmaking is 316L Stainless Steel. It is of a surgical grade, hypoallergenic and capable of being finished in multiple ways and, crucially, refinished many times without suffering significant deformation (unless it has sustained some serious damage). The “gold standard” of steel within the industry, however, is 904L, which is a type of steel used almost exclusively by Rolex (although more and more brands are moving into this territory in an attempt to disrupt Rolex’s unique selling point). 904L is said to be more resistant to corrosion, and able to hold a higher polish compared to 316L. Realistically, it is unlikely you would notice much corrosion on a 316L stainless steel watch, as the difference between the two types of steel would take a remarkable environment within which to manifest. **Yellow Gold** After more than a decade in the wilderness, yellow gold, which was almost certainly your father or grandfather’s idea of what a real watch should be made of, is making a subtle return. These days, due to the relative unpopularity of the metal amongst men, there are fewer 9 karat gold watches than there would have been a generation or two ago. Instead, most high-end yellow gold watches are made from 18 karat gold (75% pure). Some brands have experimented with new gold alloys in an attempt to contemporize a somewhat out--of-date material. Perhaps the most active (and successful) on this front, has been Hublot, which has, in their Nyon-based foundry, managed to create both “King Gold” and “Magic Gold”. The latter is fused with carbon particles and almost impossible to scratch. Due to the attempted “democratization of luxury” by many entry-level “luxury” brands, the commonness of gold-colored coatings such as PVD (powder vapor deposition) has risen. This is a cost-effective and relatively durable way to achieve the same kind of look as genuine gold. Be warned, though, it doesn’t take that trained an eye to spot the difference between a PVD coating and the real thing. Genuine yellow gold has a warmth and depth to it that is really stark when placed next to an imitation. That’s thanks to it being the purest color of all golds, which has the added benefit of making it the easiest to maintain. **Red/Rose/Pink Gold** During yellow gold’s absence, its pinkish peer took center stage. In the 1990s, rose gold was barely a thing in mainstream circles, and it was rare indeed to see it feature in watchmaking. Now, however, the warmer, perhaps more forgiving shade of gold is everywhere, and despite yellow gold’s slight return, red/rose/pink gold doesn’t seem to be under any real threat. The reddish hue of this metal is down to the amount of copper in the alloy. If you increase the amount of copper, you’ll achieve a redder result. Its Karat weight is still decided by the amount of pure gold in the mix, and the most common ratio again is 75% gold to 25% copper (by mass). That would carry an 18 karat rating, which is normally the highest karat you would expect to find as there is no such thing as pure rose gold. One of rose gold’s benefits is that the copper in the alloy makes it more durable than its yellow or white gold counterparts. However, one caveat that comes with that is that the copper means this is not a hypoallergenic metal so may cause a slight irritation to some skin types. **White Gold** One of the most divisive, and yet still incredibly common materials in watchmaking, is white gold. While it normally only makes an appearance in Haute Horlogerie, it has a large following of those who enjoy the concept of stealth luxury. Simply put, some prefer the look of steel, but want to know that they are wearing something more luxurious, despite the fact it is not immediately obvious to anyone around them. As such, white gold has exploded in popularity (in industries other than watchmaking also) and means it is far easier to coordinate your wristwatch with contemporary jewelry. One other advantage is that white gold is alloyed with tougher metals than yellow gold, which makes it more long-lasting.

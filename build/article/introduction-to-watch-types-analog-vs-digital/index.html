Introduction to Watch Types: Analog Vs Digital

February 7, 2020 1:04 PM

share

Broadly speaking, there are two types of wristwatch display. These are called analog and digital. You will hear the display itself referred to under a few different names too, most commonly the dial or the face. Simply put, it is the part of the watch which displays the time.


The dial can come in many different shapes and sizes. Dials are often bold colors to improve legibility by creating a stark contrast between the background and the foreground.

Traditional watches (and most modern luxury watches) use an analog display. Analog watches tell the time using hands and markers. The hands, which normally indicate the hours, minutes, and seconds, are long, thin sticks that extend from the center of the dial and literally point at markers set around the outside of the dial at regular intervals. The markers themselves can take many forms. Sometimes these markers are just sticks (batons), or dots on the dial (either printed or applied so they have a three-dimensional effect). Very often they are numbers. Numbers that we use everyday like 1, 2, 3, and so on, are referred to as Arabic numerals. In many traditional watches (and especially on old clocks) it was more common to use Roman numerals such as I, II, III, and so forth.


Excellent examples of digital watches can be seen from Cartier, a Parisian watchmaker founded in 1847 and now owned by the Richemont Group, a massive Swiss conglomerate that owns several brands, many of which specialize in high-end mechanical, analog watches.


<Insert Cartier Tank Image>


The Cartier Tank is regarded as an industry stalwart that recalls the Art Deco design period. Other examples of very traditional watches can be seen from luxury German watchmaker, A. Lange & Söhne, whose Saxonia Thin is the epitome of analog elegance. Many of these more traditional watches have a great deal of effort spent on the manufacture of the hands themselves, with heat blueing (a technique that requires the steel hands to be heated by a flame or in an oven to more than 500 degrees Fahrenheit before a blue oxidised layer appears on the surface) a particularly popular method of decoration. Given their importance to the watch and its function, this isn’t all that surprising. But what is it that hands actually do?


The shortest of the hands indicates the hour; the next longest indicates the minutes; the longest and thinnest indicates the seconds and ticks at least one time per second. Some very complicated have other types of hands that are capable of displaying all manner of things such as a second time zone, the date, the day, month, leap year, power reserve, whether it is day/night, elapsed time as part of a stopwatch function, and many more.


<Insert Casio Image>


Digital displays, however, do not use hands at all. Instead, they use only numbers. These numbers are arranged in the following format to indicate hours (the first two numbers), minutes (the middle two numbers), and seconds (the third pair of numbers on the right): 00:00:00.


In a digital format, it is also common for the 24-hour clock to be used (this is when the designation of AM and PM to denote before and after midday is abandoned in favor of numbers 0-24). In this format, for example, five seconds past midday would be written as follows: 12:00:05.


Digital readouts are very commonly used for cheap, quartz-powered watches. The straightforward, intuitive presentation of information also makes them excellent choices for children learning to tell the time, for fans of low-cost, highly functional technology, or for groups of people requiring incredible (and often synchronized) accuracy, such as the police or military.


One of the most commonplace (and collectible) quartz brands is Casio. Despite this Japanese brand’s immense popularity, the watches retail from just $20 and rarely break the $1,000 mark. Almost all of Casio’s watches employ digital displays, but it is becoming increasingly common to see digital and analog displays used together in the brand’s popular G-Shock range.


<Insert Breitling Aerospace Image>


While luxury digital watches are much, much rarer, there are some quartz-powered watches that fulfill the criteria to be called luxury, and some incredibly high-end companies (specifically A. Lange & Söhne again) that produce mechanically-driven digital watches. While the latter is really a novelty that was designed to titillate luxury watch collectors, the former presents an interesting value proposition. For those looking to buy a well-known brand that carries with it certain suggestions of status and wealth, but not so keen on spending over $5,000, the Breitling Aerospace – an incredibly well-thought of analog/digital quartz that has stood the test of time – is quite a tempting option.


Both formats have their fans and detractors, and each type is better at and for some things than the other. Ultimately, it comes down to personal preference, which, as you will discover, is what makes watch collecting so much fun.
